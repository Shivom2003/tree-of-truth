## Ancient & Classical

---

### Adi Shankaracharya
*c. 788–820 CE — India*

**Core question:** Is the individual self ultimately different from the ground of being — or is the sense of separation the only illusion?

**Primary contribution:** Shankaracharya took the fragmented insights of the Upanishads and built them into the most rigorous philosophical system of non-dualism the world has seen: Advaita Vedanta. His central argument — that Brahman (universal consciousness) is the only reality, and the individual self (Atman) is identical to it — is not a mystical claim but a philosophical one, argued step by step. He introduced adhyasa (superimposition) as the mechanism by which the infinite mistakenly identifies with the finite, and vivartavada to explain how apparent multiplicity arises from one undivided reality without actually changing it. His Vivekachudamani remains the clearest systematic path through the Vedantic argument.

**Key quote:** *"Brahma satyam jagan mithya, jivo brahmaiva naparah."*
Brahman alone is real. The world is appearance. The individual self is none other than Brahman.

**Recommended works:**
- *Vivekachudamani* (Swami Turiyananda translation) — the complete Vedantic argument
- *Atma Bodha* — shorter; Self-Knowledge in 68 verses
- Sarvapriyananda's commentaries (YouTube) — Shankara made accessible without loss of depth

**Spectrum position:** Deep Roots — R1 (Advaita Vedanta)

**Connect to:** R1, R3 (Direct Inquiry), B5 (Panpsychism & Idealism — modern philosophy arriving at his conclusion), Ramana leaf, Sarvapriyananda leaf

### Ramana Maharshi
*1879–1950 — India*

**Core question:** Who am I?

**Primary contribution:** Ramana did not arrive at self-inquiry through study — he arrived through a spontaneous death experience at the age of sixteen, in which he directly recognized that his true nature was not the body or the mind but the awareness within which they appeared. From that point until his death, his teaching was a single, consistent pointer: trace the I-thought back to its source. Not conceptually. Directly. His method — atma vichara — is the practice of asking "Who am I?" not to generate an answer but to find the one who is asking. He is the convergence point of the Vedantic tradition and direct phenomenological method.

**Key quote:** *"The mind will subside only by means of the enquiry 'Who am I?' The thought 'Who am I?' will destroy all other thoughts, and like the stick used for stirring the funeral pyre, will itself in the end get destroyed. Then there will arise Self-realization."*

**Recommended works:**
- *Who Am I?* — Ramana Maharshi (short; the essential method text)
- *Talks with Sri Ramana Maharshi* — dialogues in full
- *Be As You Are* — David Godman (accessible compilation)

**Spectrum position:** Deep Roots — R1, R3

**Connect to:** R1 (Advaita Vedanta), R3 (Direct Inquiry), Nisargadatta leaf, Sarvapriyananda leaf, Spira leaf

### Nisargadatta Maharaj
*1897–1981 — India*

**Core question:** What are you before you think you are something?

**Primary contribution:** A bidi-seller from Mumbai who received a single instruction from his guru — abide as the I Am — and followed it to its conclusion in three years. Nisargadatta's teaching is the most uncompromising in this project: not a path, not a gradual development, but a recognition. The I Am — the bare sense of being, prior to all content — is the last pointer. When you rest as I Am without adding "I am this" or "I am that," what remains? His dialogues in *I Am That* are considered among the most direct transmission texts in any tradition.

**Key quote:** *"You are not what you think you are. Find out what you are. The I Am is the last illusion — but it is also the door."*

**Recommended works:**
- *I Am That* — Nisargadatta Maharaj (primary text; start with Part I)
- *Prior to Consciousness* — later, more radical dialogues
- *The Ultimate Medicine* — dialogues on the final teaching

**Spectrum position:** Deep Roots — R1, R3

**Connect to:** R1, R3, Ramana leaf, Lucille leaf, Spira leaf

### Gautama Buddha
*c. 5th–4th century BCE — India/Nepal*

**Core question:** What causes suffering — and can awareness itself be liberated from it?

**Primary contribution:** The Buddha's contribution to this project is specifically the anatta (no-self) teaching: the five aggregates that constitute a person — form, sensation, perception, mental formations, and consciousness — are not a self, not mine, not what I am. When seen clearly, the suffering built on identifying with these aggregates ceases. But the teaching raises an irreducible question this project takes seriously: if there is no self, what is it that recognizes the no-self? What is aware? His teaching does not answer this directly — and that silence is as important as the teaching.

**Key quote:** *"Mind is the forerunner of all actions. All deeds are led by mind, created by mind. If one speaks or acts with a serene mind, happiness follows, as a shadow that never departs."*

**Recommended works:**
- *Dhammapada* (Eknath Easwaran translation — accessible)
- *What the Buddha Taught* — Walpola Rahula (rigorous introduction)
- *In the Buddha's Words* — Bhikkhu Bodhi (primary source anthology)

**Spectrum position:** Deep Roots — R2 (Buddhist Inquiry)

**Connect to:** R2, R3, Nagarjuna leaf, Advaita Vedanta (convergence)

### Nagarjuna
*c. 2nd century CE — India*

**Core question:** Does anything — including consciousness, including emptiness itself — have inherent existence?

**Primary contribution:** Nagarjuna's Mulamadhyamakakarika is one of the most rigorous pieces of philosophical argumentation in human history. His method: take any phenomenon — causation, motion, the self, time, nirvana — and demonstrate through logical analysis that it cannot be found to have inherent existence from its own side. Nothing exists independently. Everything arises in dependence on everything else. Crucially, emptiness (śūnyatā) is itself empty — this is not nihilism, it is the most radical openness. The Middle Way: beyond both existence and non-existence.

**Key quote:** *"Whatever is dependently co-arisen, that is explained to be emptiness. That, being a dependent designation, is itself the middle way."*

**Recommended works:**
- *Mulamadhyamakakarika* (Jay Garfield translation with commentary — essential)
- *The Fundamental Wisdom of the Middle Way* — Jay Garfield
- *Emptiness: A Practical Guide for Meditators* — Guy Armstrong (accessible application)

**Spectrum position:** Deep Roots — R2 (Buddhist Inquiry)

**Connect to:** R2, R4 (Phenomenology — convergence with the no-self), B2 (Philosophy of Mind — śūnyatā and the hard problem)

### Patanjali
*c. 2nd century BCE – 4th century CE (disputed)*

**Core question:** How does the movement of the mind obscure the nature of pure consciousness, and how can that movement be stilled?

**Primary contribution:** The Yoga Sutras — 196 aphorisms that constitute the most systematic pre-modern psychology of consciousness in any tradition. Patanjali distinguishes between the seer (purusha — pure consciousness, the witness) and the seen (prakriti — the field of nature, including the mind). The mind's fluctuations (vrittis) create the illusion of identification with what is seen. Yoga — in its original meaning — is the cessation of those fluctuations, not physical postures. His taxonomy of the samadhi states provides an experiential map of the territory beyond ordinary mind.

**Key quote:** *"Yoga chitta vritti nirodha."*
Yoga is the cessation of the fluctuations of the mind-field. Then the Seer abides in its own nature.

**Recommended works:**
- *The Yoga Sutras of Patanjali* (Swami Satchidananda translation — accessible with commentary)
- *Inside the Yoga Sutras* — Reverend Jaganath Carrera
- *The Science of Yoga* — I.K. Taimni (technical; for depth)

**Spectrum position:** Roots — R5, R1

**Connect to:** R1 (Advaita — purusha and Atman), R3 (Direct Inquiry — the witness), R5 (Esoteric Traditions — samadhi states)

### Ramakrishna Paramahamsa
*1836–1886 — India*

**Core question:** Are all genuine spiritual paths — across traditions — pointing at the same undivided reality?

**Primary contribution:** Ramakrishna did not argue for the unity of religions — he experimented with it. He practiced Advaita Vedanta, Shakta devotion, Vaishnavism, Sufism, and Christianity, in succession, reaching states of samadhi in each. His life is a phenomenological demonstration — not a theological claim — that the same ground is accessible from different directions. His influence on Swami Vivekananda, who brought Vedanta to the West, makes him the indirect source of the modern Vedantic conversation.

**Key quote:** *"As many faiths, so many paths."*

**Recommended works:**
- *The Gospel of Sri Ramakrishna* — M. (Mahendranath Gupta) — the primary account; vast but profound
- *Ramakrishna and His Disciples* — Christopher Isherwood (accessible biography)

**Spectrum position:** Roots — R1, R5

**Connect to:** R1, R5, Vivekananda leaf, Patanjali leaf

### Swami Vivekananda
*1863–1902 — India / International*

**Core question:** How can the depth of Vedantic non-dualism speak to the modern, scientific, and Western mind — without losing its radical edge?

**Primary contribution:** Brought Advaita Vedanta to the West, beginning with his address at the Parliament of the World's Religions in Chicago in 1893. More importantly for this project: he framed Vedanta in terms that could engage scientific and philosophical modernity — not as religion but as the science of consciousness. His four yogas (karma, jnana, raja, bhakti) as paths suited to different temperaments. His *Raja Yoga* remains the most accessible philosophical Vedanta in the English language.

**Key quote:** *"You are not sinners — you are gods. The Atman is the witness, the eternal consciousness behind all states. Arise, awake, and stop not till the goal is reached."*

**Recommended works:**
- *Raja Yoga* — Swami Vivekananda
- *Jnana Yoga* — Swami Vivekananda
- *The Complete Works of Swami Vivekananda* (selected — available free online)

**Spectrum position:** Roots, Bridge to modern — R1

**Connect to:** R1, R3, Ramakrishna leaf, Sarvapriyananda leaf (lineage continuation)

## Modern Scientists & Philosophers

---

### Federico Faggin
*1941– — Italy / USA*

**Core question:** Can consciousness — with its qualia, free will, and meaning — be reduced to computation?

**Primary contribution:** Faggin invented the microprocessor (the Intel 4004, 1971) — the foundational technology of the digital age. Decades later, through his own scientific reasoning, he concluded that consciousness cannot be what computation produces, because consciousness has properties that are irreducibly non-computational: qualia (the felt quality of experience), free will, and semantic meaning. His book *Irreducible* argues this from first principles, then proposes a quantum framework for how consciousness might be fundamental. His journey — from the architect of computing to consciousness researcher — is itself the argument.

**Key quote:** *"Consciousness is not what the brain does. It is what the brain is an expression of."*

**Recommended works:**
- *Irreducible* — Federico Faggin (2021) — primary text
- His interviews at the Galileo Commission and Science & Nonduality (SAND) conference

**Spectrum position:** Branches → Roots convergence — B4, B5

**Connect to:** B4 (Quantum Mind), B5 (Panpsychism), Hoffman leaf, Kastrup leaf

### Donald Hoffman
*1955– — USA*

**Core question:** Is the world we perceive the world as it actually is — or a user interface shaped by fitness, not truth?

**Primary contribution:** Hoffman applied evolutionary game theory to perception and reached a radical conclusion: organisms that perceive reality accurately are consistently out-competed by organisms that perceive fitness payoffs. Our perceptions are a user interface — like desktop icons — not windows into reality. What lies behind the interface? His Conscious Agents Theory proposes a mathematical framework: reality is a network of interacting conscious agents, not physical objects. His work converges with Kastrup's idealism and Vedantic māyā from a purely scientific starting point.

**Key quote:** *"Evolution has shaped us with perceptions that allow us to survive. But part of that involves hiding from us the stuff we don't need to know. And that's pretty much all of reality."*

**Recommended works:**
- *The Case Against Reality* — Donald Hoffman (2019)
- TED Talk: "Do We See Reality as It Is?" (2015) — accessible entry
- "Objects of Consciousness" — Hoffman (2015 paper)

**Spectrum position:** Branches → Roots — B4, B5

**Connect to:** B4, B5, Faggin leaf, Kastrup leaf, Advaita Vedanta (māyā convergence)

### Bernardo Kastrup
*1973– — Netherlands*

**Core question:** Why is idealism — consciousness as the ground of all existence — the most parsimonious and coherent account of reality?

**Primary contribution:** Kastrup has done more than anyone to rehabilitate idealism as a serious philosophical position in contemporary analytic philosophy. His Analytical Idealism: consciousness is all there is; the physical world is its extrinsic appearance; individual minds are dissociated alters of a universal consciousness — analogous, at cosmic scale, to dissociative identity disorder. His argument is rigorous and directly engages the standard materialist objections. He draws explicit parallels between analytical idealism and Advaita Vedanta, arguing they are the same position arrived at by different routes.

**Key quote:** *"The world is in mind, not mind in the world. What we call matter is the extrinsic appearance of mental processes we cannot introspect from the outside."*

**Recommended works:**
- *The Idea of the World* — Bernardo Kastrup (the rigorous philosophical case)
- *Why Materialism Is Baloney* — accessible introduction
- *More Than Allegory* — on myth, consciousness, and meaning

**Spectrum position:** Branches → Roots convergence — B5

**Connect to:** B5, B2, Hoffman leaf, Goff leaf, R1 (Advaita — structural identity)

### David Chalmers
*1966– — Australia / USA*

**Core question:** Why is there something it is like to have an experience?

**Primary contribution:** Formulated the "hard problem of consciousness" (1995) — the distinction that changed the philosophy of mind. Easy problems: explaining attention, memory, sleep, behavior. Hard problem: explaining why any of these functions is *accompanied by subjective experience* at all. Why doesn't the processing happen "in the dark," without any felt quality? Chalmers showed this is not a problem of insufficient data — it is a problem of kind. His work legitimized consciousness as a serious, irreducible philosophical problem after decades of dismissal. More recently, he has moved toward panpsychism.

**Key quote:** *"Why is it that when our cognitive systems engage in visual and auditory information-processing, we have visual or auditory experience? Why doesn't all of this information-processing go on 'in the dark,' without any accompanying experience?"*

**Recommended works:**
- "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness" — Chalmers (1995 paper — the defining formulation)
- *The Conscious Mind* — David Chalmers
- *Reality+* — his most recent work (virtual reality and the nature of reality)

**Spectrum position:** Deep Branches — B2, B5

**Connect to:** B2, B5, Goff leaf, Koch leaf, Kastrup leaf

### Philip Goff
*1978– — UK*

**Core question:** What if consciousness was never something to be explained by physics — but something physics was always built on top of?

**Primary contribution:** Goff's central argument in *Galileo's Error*: Galileo's methodological choice to exclude qualities (consciousness, color, sound as experienced) from the scientific description of the world — keeping only quantities — was a productive choice for physics, but it built the hard problem in from the start. Panpsychism — the view that consciousness is a fundamental feature of reality, present at all levels — is the natural resolution. His work is rigorous, clear, and directly addresses the standard objections. He is currently the most publicly prominent philosopher of panpsychism.

**Key quote:** *"The hard problem of consciousness is not a problem about consciousness. It's a problem about matter — about why our purely quantitative conception of matter leaves no room for the qualities of experience."*

**Recommended works:**
- *Galileo's Error* — Philip Goff (2019) — most accessible entry
- *Consciousness and Fundamental Reality* — the technical philosophical case
- His podcast *Mind Chat* (with Keith Frankish — the debate itself is valuable)

**Spectrum position:** Branches → Roots — B5

**Connect to:** B5, B2, Chalmers leaf, Kastrup leaf

### Christof Koch
*1956– — Germany / USA*

**Core question:** What are the neural correlates of consciousness — and what do decades of searching for them reveal about the nature of consciousness itself?

**Primary contribution:** Koch spent decades as Francis Crick's closest collaborator on the neuroscience of consciousness — the empirical project of finding what brain states are jointly sufficient for conscious experience. His journey is as significant as his findings: beginning as a confident reductionist, he gradually moved toward Integrated Information Theory (IIT) and panpsychism as the only frameworks that could account for what neuroscience was finding. A rigorous scientist who followed the evidence away from materialism.

**Key quote:** *"Consciousness is the central fact of your life. You can doubt the existence of an external world, you can doubt other minds, you can doubt many things. But you cannot doubt your own consciousness."*

**Recommended works:**
- *The Feeling of Life Itself* — Christof Koch (IIT and the science of consciousness)
- *Consciousness: Confessions of a Romantic Reductionist* — his personal intellectual journey

**Spectrum position:** Branches — B1, B5

**Connect to:** B1, B5, B2, Chalmers leaf

### Deepak Chopra
*1946– — India / USA*

**Core question:** How do consciousness, healing, and the nature of reality intersect — and how can these ideas reach people who need them most?

**Primary contribution:** Chopra is a bridge figure — the thinker who brought Vedantic and mind-body ideas to a mass audience that would not otherwise have encountered them. His academic critics are right that he sometimes overextends quantum language; they are wrong to dismiss the underlying intuition, which is grounded in genuine Vedantic understanding. His contribution to this project is specifically as a gateway: millions of people first encountered the idea that consciousness might be fundamental through his work. Presented here honestly — as an accessible entry point, not a primary philosophical source.

**Key quote:** *"Consciousness is the ground of all being. The universe is not made of dead matter; it is made of living consciousness."*

**Recommended works:**
- *Quantum Healing* (with the caveat that the quantum language is metaphorical)
- *You Are the Universe* — Chopra & Menas Kafatos (more rigorous)
- His conversations with Kastrup and Hoffman (YouTube — the more rigorous side of his work)

**Spectrum position:** Bridge — R1 → B1 (accessible entry point)

**Connect to:** R1, B5, Kastrup leaf, Faggin leaf

### Thomas Metzinger
*1958– — Germany*

**Core question:** What is the self — and what happens when the brain's model of the self breaks down?

**Primary contribution:** Metzinger's phenomenal self-model (PSM) theory: the self is not an entity — it is a self-representing process, a model the brain generates of itself. The sense of being a unified, embodied subject is an internally generated representation, not a direct window onto an existing self. When the PSM is destabilized — in meditation, out-of-body experiences, certain psychedelic states, some psychiatric conditions — the sense of being a separate self dissolves or distorts. His work is the most rigorous bridge between phenomenology and the neuroscience of self-experience. He arrives at a no-self conclusion through purely scientific means — without any traditional framework.

**Key quote:** *"No such things as selves exist in the world. Nobody ever was or had a self. All that ever existed were conscious self-models that nobody owned."*

**Recommended works:**
- *The Ego Tunnel* — Metzinger (accessible)
- *Being No One* — Metzinger (advanced; the full technical theory)
- His talks at the Mind & Life Institute (YouTube)

**Spectrum position:** Bridge — R4, B3

**Connect to:** R4 (Phenomenology), B3 (Cognitive Science), R2 (Buddhist anatta convergence)

## Contemporary Teachers & Integrators

---

### Sarvapriyananda
*1969– — India / USA*

**Core question:** How can the teachings of the Upanishads — specifically Advaita Vedanta — be conveyed with full philosophical rigour and zero dilution to the modern mind?

**Primary contribution:** The most important contemporary English-language teacher of Advaita Vedanta for this project. His lecture series on the Mandukya Upanishad, the Drk-Drsya Viveka, and other Vedantic texts at the Vedanta Society of New York are freely available and represent the fullest rigorous treatment of Vedantic epistemology in English. He makes the deep sleep argument, the three-states analysis, the witness methodology, and the mahavakyas available without simplification, using examples a contemporary audience can follow.

**Key quote:** *"The Witness is never the witnessed. Consciousness is never an object. That which is aware cannot itself be the object of awareness — it is the Subject of all subjects."*

**Recommended works:**
- Mandukya Upanishad lecture series (YouTube — Vedanta Society of New York)
- Drk-Drsya Viveka lectures (YouTube)
- "Who Am I?" lecture series (YouTube)

**Spectrum position:** Deep Roots, Bridge to modern — R1, R3

**Connect to:** R1, R3, Ramana leaf, Nisargadatta leaf, Shankaracharya leaf

### Rupert Spira
*1960– — UK*

**Core question:** What is the nature of experience itself — and what does careful attention to experience reveal about what we ultimately are?

**Primary contribution:** Spira articulates the non-dual understanding with unusual philosophical clarity and felt immediacy. His inquiry method — "being aware of being aware" — is a direct, accessible pointer that does not require any traditional background. His primary argument: we already know that we are aware; the question is only whether that awareness is a property of the body-mind or its ground. His books and retreats build this case patiently, without assumption. His work bridges the direct transmission of Nisargadatta with the philosophical clarity the Western mind needs.

**Key quote:** *"You are not inside the body. The body is inside you — inside awareness. Awareness is not a product of experience. Experience is a movement within awareness."*

**Recommended works:**
- *The Nature of Consciousness* — Rupert Spira (philosophical)
- *Being Aware of Being Aware* — direct and brief
- *Presence* Vol. I & II — the full teaching

**Spectrum position:** Roots, Bridge to contemporary — R3

**Connect to:** R3, R1, Nisargadatta leaf, Lucille leaf

### Francis Lucille
*1944– — France / USA*

**Core question:** What is the direct recognition of our true nature — and how does that recognition dissolve the felt sense of separation?

**Primary contribution:** Lucille is Rupert Spira's teacher, and the lineage connection runs through Jean Klein to Atmananda Krishna Menon — one of the most philosophically rigorous lines of non-dual transmission. His approach is Socratic and precise: he does not give answers so much as help the questioner see what they are already assuming and what remains when that assumption is released. His work is less widely known than Spira's but represents the philosophical root of that lineage.

**Key quote:** *"You are not the one who has experiences. You are the experiencing itself. Not a subject among objects, but the knowing presence in which all objects and subjects appear."*

**Recommended works:**
- *Eternity Now* — Francis Lucille
- *Truth Love Beauty* — Lucille
- *The Perfume of Silence* — Lucille

**Spectrum position:** Deep Roots — R3

**Connect to:** R3, Spira leaf, Nisargadatta leaf

### Dr. Alok Kanojia (HealthyGamer)
*1985– — India / USA*

**Core question:** How can psychiatry, neuroscience, and contemplative practices be integrated to heal modern mental health crises?

**Primary contribution:** Psychiatrist, neurobiology-focused clinician, and founder of HealthyGamer — a platform reaching millions of young people struggling with identity, purpose, and mental health. Kanojia is unique in this list: he is neither a philosopher nor a traditional teacher, but a clinician who has integrated neuroscience and psychiatric frameworks with contemplative practices. His contribution is demonstrating that combining modern scientific understanding of the brain with ancient attentional training directly addresses the lived suffering of modern people.

**Key quote:** *"The mind is not broken — it is a tool we were never taught to use. And the ancient traditions knew this."*

**Recommended works:**
- HealthyGamer YouTube channel — specifically the neuroscience of addiction and ego discussions
- His interview series on consciousness and mental health

**Spectrum position:** Bridge — B1 (Neuroscience) → practical application / mental health

**Connect to:** B1 (Neuroscience), R3, Fruit section (practices), Vivekananda leaf
